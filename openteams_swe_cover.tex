Hi OpenTeams, my name is Giordon Stark, and I am what you might call a ``licensed quantum mechanic.'' Over the past decade I have balanced the trifecta that rattles my brain: physics, software, and hardware. As you have probably guessed from this letter, I am considering a transition from academia into industry, and your Engineer/Senior Engineer, Packaging role caught my eye, -in no small part because OpenTeams treats open source as an ethos rather than a marketing line. I believe I am a strong fit. You list packaging for Conda, compiled build systems, cross-platform reliability, CI/CD, scientific computing, and client- and community-facing communication; these are, more or less, the contents of my week.

I am a core developer of pyhf, the library that made ATLAS the first particle-physics experiment to publish full statistical likelihoods, and a co-administrator of the Scikit-HEP organization. A large part of that work is the unglamorous-but-essential packaging layer: shipping libraries to both PyPI and conda-forge and keeping them installable across Linux, macOS, and Windows, so that a graduate student on an unfamiliar machine can \texttt{pip install} or \texttt{conda install} and have it simply work. Scientific Python is not pure Python; it rests on a mountain of compiled C/C++/Fortran that has to be built, linked, and distributed correctly, and the gap between ``compiles on my Linux box'' and ``installs cleanly on a colleague's Windows laptop'' is exactly where I spend my time.

Most of that effort runs through HEP Packaging Coordination, a community project I help run that now distributes more than 100 high-energy-physics tools on conda-forge. I author and maintain feedstocks for some genuinely unpleasant compiled software such as ROOT, Geant4, Pythia8, and friends; working with CMake and Meson builds, compilers, and linkers into reproducible cross-platform packages, and reporting fixes upstream rather than routing around them. I lean heavily on pixi to make those environments reproducible day to day, and I have spent years inside CI/CD pipelines for build, test, and release automation; I also helped drag a 5000-person collaboration out of Subversion into Git and GitLab, then taught the three-hour CI/CD crash course to make the migration stick.

The part of this role I am most drawn to is the client- and community-facing piece. In ATLAS, everyone who contributes for a year becomes an author, which means every analysis I led was circulated to thousands of people with opinions on my uncertainties, my methods, and whether my color choices insulted their sensibilities. I learned early that feedback is a gift, and that the job is half engineering and half making sure nobody feels like they are taking a backseat. I treat software literacy as something everyone deserves access to, which is why documentation, teaching, and upstream communication are part of my ethos

In short, I would be a good fit: I pick up new tools quickly, I handle steep learning curves, and I care about the open-source health of the things I ship. There are plenty of other quirky skills that do not fit in a cover letter such as baking sourdough and pavlovas, the various languages (programming or otherwise) I have dabbled in, an apparently unkillable instinct for finding bugs, but feel free to ask. I hope to hear from you soon.
